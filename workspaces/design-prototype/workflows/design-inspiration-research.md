# Design Inspiration Research Workflow

## 1. Purpose

Research design references and convert them into original, product-specific design principles and directions.

## 2. When to Use

Use before defining visual direction, redesigning a page, creating a high-fidelity prototype, or building a design system.

## 3. Inputs

- Product goal
- Target user
- Platform
- Scenario
- Brand keywords
- Competitor or reference category
- Current design problem

## 4. Steps

1. Clarify the design question and product context.
2. Generate search keywords from product category, user task, platform, visual mood, and interaction pattern.
3. Use sources such as Figma Community, Dribbble, Mobbin, Awwwards, Behance, Pinterest, Apple Human Interface Guidelines, Material Design, Linear, Notion, Raycast, Arc, Perplexity, ChatGPT, and Framer.
4. Analyze references by visual style, interaction pattern, information architecture, component pattern, and product fit.
5. Identify what to learn and what not to copy.
6. Translate patterns into an original design direction for this product.
7. Produce a direction that can become a Figma Prompt or prototype brief.

## 5. Outputs

| Reference | Visual Style | Interaction Pattern | What to Learn | What Not to Copy | How to Adapt |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
|  |  |  |  |  |  |

## 6. Quality Checklist

- References are analyzed as patterns, not copied as final design.
- Output includes visual, interaction, and information architecture insights.
- Adaptation is tied to current product goals and users.
- Latest external references are verified and sourced when used.
- Final direction is specific enough for design execution.

## 7. Common Mistakes

- Copying a popular visual style without product fit.
- Using Dribbble-style shots as full product UX.
- Ignoring platform conventions.
- Listing references without adaptation.
- Forgetting what not to copy.
